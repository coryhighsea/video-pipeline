## HOOK (0:00–0:30)

Germany had a deadline.

On March 6th, 2026, companies in scope for NIS2 were required to have registered with the BSI — Germany's national cybersecurity authority.

Around 30,000 companies were expected to register.

Roughly 11,000 did.

That's about one in three.

So either Germany has a compliance crisis on its hands — or this number tells a more complicated story. I want to look at both possibilities in this video, because I think the honest answer is somewhere in the middle — and it matters for anyone running a business in Germany right now.

## WHAT REGISTRATION ACTUALLY IS (0:30–1:30)

Before we talk about what that number means, let's be clear about what registration actually is — because it's a common point of confusion.

Registering with the BSI is not a compliance audit. It's not a certification. It's not proof that your company is NIS2-compliant.

It's a form. Your company name, the sector you operate in, your entity classification — whether you're a "wichtige" or "besonders wichtige Einrichtung", an important or essential entity — and a designated contact person for security incidents.

That's it. It takes maybe 30 minutes if you have the information in front of you.

So when we say 11,000 companies registered by March 6th — we're saying 11,000 companies completed a 30-minute form. And 19,000 didn't.

That framing matters. Because if companies haven't done the simplest step — registration — you can make some reasonable inferences about where they are on the deeper compliance work.

---

## WHY SO FEW? THE HONEST ANSWER (1:30–4:00)

There are real reasons why registration numbers are low — and I want to go through them honestly, because I think lazy analysis either blames companies unfairly or lets them off the hook too easily.

**Reason one: Awareness is genuinely low.**

NIS2 was implemented in Germany through the BSIG in December 2025. That's not long ago. The BSI registration portal didn't open until January 2026. Companies had a roughly two-month window to learn about the law, determine if they were in scope, and complete registration.

For a large enterprise with a legal and compliance team — two months is tight but manageable. For a mid-sized company in the food sector or waste management that has never thought about EU cybersecurity law — two months is almost nothing.

A lot of companies in scope genuinely don't know they're in scope. NIS2 reaches into sectors that have never historically had cybersecurity regulation — logistics companies, research institutions, certain manufacturers. These aren't companies that read BSI guidance in their spare time.

**Reason two: Scope uncertainty.**

One of the most common questions we hear is: "Are we actually affected?"

The two-part test — 50 or more employees, or 10 million euros in revenue, operating in one of 18 sectors — sounds simple. In practice there are grey areas. Subsidiaries of multinationals. Companies that touch a regulated sector as part of a broader business. Companies unsure whether their activity counts as "digital infrastructure" or not.

The BSI has published guidance, but guidance requires someone to read it, interpret it, and make a decision. That's friction. And friction creates delay.

**Reason three: Deliberate wait-and-see.**

Some companies know they're in scope. They've decided to wait and see what enforcement actually looks like before investing time and money in compliance.

This is a real calculation that real companies make. And on a pure short-term risk basis, it's not irrational — enforcement takes time to ramp up, and no company has been fined yet under the German BSIG.

But it's a bet. And it's worth being clear about what you're betting on.

**Reason four: The deeper work feels overwhelming.**

Registration is the first step. But companies looking ahead see 132 requirements across 12 categories, the word "audit," personal liability for management, and fines up to 10 million euros. And some of them freeze.

They delay registration not because registration is hard — but because registration feels like the first domino in a very long chain they're not ready to start knocking over.

That's a psychological barrier more than a practical one. But it's real.

## WHAT THE BSI ACTUALLY DOES NOW (4:00–5:30)

So — what happens to the 19,000 companies that didn't register?

Honestly, the answer right now is: not much immediately.

The BSI is not going to send fine notices to 19,000 companies in the weeks after a deadline. Enforcement infrastructure — the processes, the staffing, the legal procedures for issuing sanctions — takes time to build. Germany is still in the early stages of that.

But a few things are worth understanding about how this is likely to play out.

First — the registration data is the BSI's starting point for supervision. They now have a list of companies that registered. Companies that are conspicuously absent from that list, operating in regulated sectors, are exactly the companies that will attract scrutiny as enforcement develops.

Second — fines under the BSIG are significant. Essential entities — "besonders wichtige Einrichtungen" — face fines up to 10 million euros or 2% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Important entities — "wichtige Einrichtungen" — face up to 7 million euros or 1.4% of global turnover. And critically, management can be held personally liable. That's not a fine on the company — that's a fine on the individual.

Third — the BSI does have supervisory powers. They can request documentation. They can conduct audits. They can issue binding instructions. These powers exist now, and they will be used — the question is pace, not direction.

The honest read: we're in an early enforcement window. That window will close.

---

## WHAT SHOULD NON-REGISTERED COMPANIES DO? (5:30–7:00)

If your company is in scope and hasn't registered — what should you actually do?

First, don't assume you're off the hook because the deadline passed. Late registration is better than no registration. The BSI portal is still open. There is no formal grace period announced, but there's also no indication the portal closes. Register now.

Second, don't let the full compliance picture paralyze you on the easy steps. Registration is not a commitment to having everything figured out. It's a form. Do it.

Third, make a realistic assessment of where you are. The 12 categories of NIS2 compliance aren't all equal. Some — like governance and risk management — require decisions and documentation. Others — like access controls — you probably already have in practice and just need to document. A gap analysis will show you what you actually have versus what you need.

Fourth, understand what documented compliance means. NIS2 doesn't require perfect security. It requires documented, evidenced security practices. A company with a well-documented but imperfect risk management process is in a better position than a company with excellent security and nothing written down. The documentation is the actual work.

And fifth — if you genuinely don't know where to start — that's a normal position. Most companies in scope right now are in the same place. This isn't a problem unique to Germany, but the scale of non-registration in Germany suggests it's particularly acute here.

---

## WHAT THIS TELLS US (7:00–8:00)

Stepping back — what does the 11,000 number actually tell us?

I think it tells us that large-scale regulatory rollouts into new sectors are hard. NIS2 is asking companies that have never been cybersecurity-regulated to behave like regulated entities. That's a cultural shift, not just a compliance task. And cultural shifts don't happen because a deadline passed.

It also tells us that the gap between a law being passed and a law being meaningfully enforced creates a window where non-compliance feels safe. That window is always temporary.

Germany isn't uniquely bad at this — similar patterns played out in other EU member states with NIS2 implementation. But Germany has a large number of Mittelstand companies in exactly the sectors NIS2 targets, and the reach of this law into those companies is significant.

The 11,000 companies that registered are ahead. The 19,000 that didn't have work to do — and the longer they wait, the more uncomfortable the catch-up becomes.
