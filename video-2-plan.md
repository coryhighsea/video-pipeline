## Topic: "NIS2 Personal Liability — Why CEOs Are Now Personally Responsible"

### Why this topic

Video 1 answered "what is NIS2?" — the foundational explainer.

Video 2 needs a hook that creates **urgency for decision-makers**. Personal liability does that.

Most LinkedIn viewers are CEOs, CTOs, or compliance officers. "Your company might get fined" lands okay. "You personally might be fined or removed from your position" stops the scroll.

Key facts that make this compelling:
- NIS2 explicitly names management bodies as personally liable
- Fines: up to **€10M or 2% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher)
- Management can be **temporarily banned** from leadership roles
- This is new — GDPR fined companies, NIS2 fines people

## Talk Track / Script Outline

### Intro (0:00–0:45)

> "Today I want to talk about something that most NIS2 articles skip over — personal liability. Because NIS2 doesn't just create obligations for your company. It creates personal obligations for management. For you. And if your company is non-compliant, you can be held personally accountable. Let me break down exactly what that means and what you need to do about it."

### Section 1: What Changed With NIS2 (0:45–2:00)
*Section card: "What Changed" / The Shift*

> "With GDPR, fines went to the company. That was uncomfortable, but manageable — you could budget for risk, take out insurance, treat it as a cost of doing business. NIS2 changes the game. Under NIS2, management bodies — meaning your board of directors, your C-suite — are personally responsible for approving and overseeing cybersecurity risk management measures. So if something goes wrong and your company wasn't compliant, it's not just the company that gets investigated. It's you."

**Stat callout: €10M** (or 2% of global turnover — whichever is higher)

### Section 2: Who Is "Management" Under NIS2? (2:00–3:00)
*Section card: "Who Is Liable?" / Management Bodies*

> "The directive specifically refers to 'management bodies.' In practice this means: your CEO, your board of directors, your managing directors, your C-level leadership. Not just the CISO. Not just the IT team. The people at the top. And the directive says they must complete cybersecurity training — they must understand the risks the organization faces. They can't just delegate this down and wash their hands of it. Ignorance is not a defense."

**Stat callout: Temporary ban** from management roles as a possible sanction

### Section 3: What Happens When You're Non-Compliant (3:00–4:15)
*Section card: "The Consequences" / What Auditors Can Do*

> "So what actually happens? Your national authority — in Germany that's the BSI — can investigate your company. They can audit you, on-site or remotely. And if they find you're not compliant, they have a range of enforcement tools. Fines, yes. But also: orders to implement specific security measures. Public disclosure of violations. And for essential entities — that means the more critical sectors — they can temporarily prohibit individuals from exercising management functions. That's a temporary ban on you personally doing your job. And that's new. That's NIS2."

**Stat callouts:**
- €10M / 2% — essential entities
- €7M / 1.4% — important entities

### Section 4: The Three Things Management Must Do (4:15–5:30)
*Section card: "Your Obligations" / What You Must Do*

> "So given all that, what does management actually need to do? Three things. First: approve your cybersecurity risk management measures. You can't just sign off on a policy someone else wrote. You need to understand it. Second: oversee the implementation. Make sure it's actually happening — not just on paper. Third: ensure your organization completes the required compliance steps. The 132 requirements, the 12 categories, all of it. And you need to be able to prove all of this — because when auditors come, they will ask to see the evidence. If you can't show it, it's the same as not having done it."

**Stat callouts:**
- 132 requirements
- 12 categories

### Section 5: How to Protect Yourself (5:30–6:30)
*Section card: "How to Protect Yourself" / Action*

> "The good news: this is manageable. The companies that get into trouble are the ones that haven't started. The ones that are compliant have a documented, auditable process in place. They know who is responsible for each requirement. They have an evidence trail. They have incident response protocols ready before an incident happens. That's what protects you personally — having the receipts. At nisd2.eu we built a platform specifically to help management teams walk through each requirement, assign ownership, and build that audit trail. You can sign up for free today and see exactly where your gaps are."
