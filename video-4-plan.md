## Topic: "NIS2 Supply Chain Security — Your Vendors Are Your Problem"

### Why this topic

Videos 1–3 cover the overview, personal liability, and incident response. Video 4 goes deep on supply chain — the most underestimated NIS2 obligation.

Most companies assume NIS2 is an internal compliance exercise. It's not. Under NIS2, you are responsible for the security of every supplier in your chain. If your vendor gets breached and that breach hits you — NIS2 treats *your* security posture as inadequate.

This is the SolarWinds problem codified into law. It speaks directly to procurement teams, CTOs, and any company with significant vendor relationships (which is every company in scope). Strong LinkedIn angle because supply chain risk is one of the top board-level concerns in 2025.

## Talk Track / Script Outline

### Intro (0:00–0:45)

> "Here's a question most companies don't ask when they start NIS2 compliance: what are my vendors doing about it? Because under NIS2, that's your problem. Not theirs. Yours. If a supplier in your chain gets breached and that breach propagates to you, NIS2 doesn't care that it wasn't your fault. It asks: did you assess the security of your supply chain? Did you have agreements in place? Did you manage the risk? If you didn't — you're non-compliant. Today I want to talk about what NIS2 actually requires for supply chain security, and what you need to do about your vendors."

### Section 1: Why Supply Chain Is in NIS2 (0:45–1:45)
*Section card: "Why Supply Chain?" / The Reason*

> "The EU didn't add supply chain requirements to NIS2 randomly. They added them because of incidents like SolarWinds — where attackers compromised a trusted software vendor and used that access to hit thousands of organizations. The lesson was clear: your security is only as good as the weakest link in your supply chain. And companies were not assessing their suppliers. They were trusting them. NIS2 changes that. It requires entities in scope to assess the security posture of their suppliers and service providers, factor that into their own risk management, and where possible, require suppliers to meet minimum security standards."

**Stat callout: SolarWinds affected 18,000+ organizations via one vendor**

### Section 2: What NIS2 Actually Requires From You (1:45–3:00)
*Section card: "Your Obligations" / Supply Chain Requirements*

> "So what specifically does NIS2 say? Three concrete things. First: you must assess the security practices of your suppliers. That means understanding what security measures they have in place — not just asking, but documenting it. Second: you must consider those assessments in your own risk management. If a supplier has poor security, that's a risk you carry. You need to account for it. Third: where possible, you should include security requirements in contracts with your suppliers. So that they're contractually obligated to maintain certain standards. Now — NIS2 doesn't give you a specific supplier questionnaire or a minimum score. It leaves the methodology to you. But you need to show you did it. Auditors will ask."

**Stat callouts:**
- 3 supply chain obligations
- Documented assessment required

### Section 3: How to Actually Assess a Supplier (3:00–4:15)
*Section card: "How to Assess" / Practical Steps*

> "In practice, supply chain security assessment for NIS2 comes down to four questions you need to be able to answer for each significant supplier. One: do they have their own security certifications? ISO 27001, SOC 2, or equivalent — these give you a baseline signal. Two: have they had security incidents in the past 12 to 24 months, and how did they handle them? Three: what data or systems of yours can they access, and is that access appropriately restricted? Four: do your contracts include breach notification requirements — so if they get hit, you find out immediately? You don't need to audit every vendor. Prioritize by risk: suppliers with access to your critical systems or sensitive data get the deepest assessment."

**Stat callouts:**
- 4 questions per supplier
- Prioritize by risk level

### Section 4: The Ripple Effect — You're a Vendor Too (4:15–5:15)
*Section card: "You're a Vendor Too" / Flip Side*

> "Here's the angle most companies miss. If you're in scope for NIS2, there's a good chance your customers are also doing NIS2 compliance — and they're going to send you a supplier security questionnaire. You are someone else's supply chain risk. So this goes both ways. Becoming NIS2 compliant yourself is also how you protect and strengthen your customer relationships. It's a competitive advantage. When a procurement team asks 'are you NIS2 compliant?' and you can say yes with documentation — that's a differentiator. Companies that can't answer that question are going to start losing contracts with larger, compliant customers."

**Stat callout: NIS2 compliance as competitive differentiator**

### Section 5: Build a Simple Supplier Register (5:15–6:30)
*Section card: "Build Your Register" / Action*

> "The practical starting point is a supplier register. A list of every vendor with access to your systems or data, ranked by risk level. For each one: their contact, what access they have, what security evidence you've collected, and when you last reviewed it. That register is what you'll show auditors. It doesn't need to be complicated — a structured document or spreadsheet works to start. The key is that you've thought through it, documented it, and you review it regularly. Sign up free at nisd2.eu and start mapping your supplier risk today."
