## Topic: "NIS2 Incident Response — You Have 72 Hours"

### Why this topic

Videos 1 & 2 cover the "what" and the "who's accountable." This video covers the most time-pressured, concrete obligation in all of NIS2: incident reporting.

"72 hours to notify the government of a cyberattack" is an extremely strong hook — most people don't know this rule exists, and most companies have no plan for it. It's practical, urgent, and directly shows why you need a system in place *before* something goes wrong.

Great for LinkedIn because it speaks to both technical and executive audiences: IT leaders need to build the process, management needs to approve it.

## Talk Track / Script Outline

### Intro (0:00–0:45)

> "Here's a scenario. Your company gets hit by a ransomware attack on a Monday morning. Your systems are encrypted, your team is scrambling. What do you do? Under NIS2, the clock is already ticking. You have 24 hours to send an early warning to the government. 72 hours to submit a full incident notification. And 30 days to deliver a final report. If you don't have a process ready before the incident happens, you're going to miss those deadlines. And missing them is itself a violation. Today I want to walk you through exactly what NIS2 requires for incident response — and what you need to have in place."

### Section 1: What Counts as a "Significant Incident"? (0:45–1:45)
*Section card: "What Is an Incident?" / Scope*

> "First — not every IT issue triggers NIS2 reporting. The directive uses the term 'significant incident.' An incident is significant if it causes, or is capable of causing, severe disruption to your services. Or if it affects other organizations or other member states. So a minor outage? Probably not. A ransomware attack that takes your systems down for 12 hours? Yes. A breach that exposes customer data and could affect your clients? Yes. The key word is 'capable of causing' — you don't need confirmed damage. If the potential is there, the clock starts."

**Stat callout: "capable of causing" severe disruption → clock starts**

### Section 2: The Three-Stage Reporting Timeline (1:45–3:15)
*Section card: "The Timeline" / Three Deadlines*

> "Once you've identified a significant incident, here's exactly what NIS2 requires. Stage one: within 24 hours of becoming aware of the incident, you must submit an early warning to your national authority. In Germany that's the BSI. This doesn't need to be a full report — it's a heads-up. You're saying: we know something happened, here's the basic situation. Stage two: within 72 hours, you must submit a full incident notification. Now you need details. What happened? What systems were affected? What's the impact? What steps have you taken? Stage three: within 30 days of the incident, you submit a final report. Root cause, full technical analysis, long-term remediation. Three stages. Three hard deadlines. All measured from the moment you become aware — not the moment you've fully investigated."

**Stat callouts:**
- 24h — early warning
- 72h — full notification
- 30d — final report

### Section 3: Why Most Companies Will Fail This (3:15–4:15)
*Section card: "The Problem" / Why Companies Fail*

> "Here's the issue. Most companies, when a cyberattack hits, spend the first 24 to 48 hours just trying to understand what happened. Forensics. Containment. Keeping the business running. And then someone remembers: we need to notify the BSI. And they have no process for doing that. No template. No assigned responsible person. No escalation chain. So they scramble, they send something incomplete, they miss the deadline, or they send the wrong level of detail. And now they have a violation on top of an incident. NIS2 doesn't give you time to figure out the process during the crisis. You have to have it ready."

**Stat callout: Most breaches take 24–48h just to understand** (set expectation)

### Section 4: What Your Incident Response Plan Must Contain (4:15–5:30)
*Section card: "Your IR Plan" / What to Prepare*

> "So what does a NIS2-compliant incident response plan look like? At minimum, five things. First: a clear definition of what a 'significant incident' means for your organization specifically — so your team can make that call fast. Second: a designated contact and backup for BSI notifications — someone whose job it is to send that report. Third: a notification template pre-filled with your company details so you're not starting from scratch at 2am. Fourth: an internal escalation chain — who gets called, in what order. Fifth: a log. Because you need to be able to prove you met the deadlines. What was sent, when, by whom. That log is your protection. If you have those five things before an incident, you will meet the reporting obligations. If you don't, you probably won't."

**Stat callouts:**
- 5 components of an IR plan
- 132 requirements (incident handling is one of 12 categories)

### Section 5: Build It Before You Need It (5:30–6:30)
*Section card: "Start Now" / Action*

> "The worst time to build an incident response process is during an incident. You need to build it when nothing is wrong, test it, assign the roles, make sure everyone knows what they're doing. And then when something goes wrong — and something always eventually goes wrong — you're ready. At nisd2.eu we have the incident handling requirements mapped out, with templates and ownership assignment built in. You can work through the entire incident response category, assign your responsible persons, and have your plan documented and auditable. Sign up free at nisd2.eu and get your incident response process in place before you need it."
