## Post 1 — What is NIS2? (Overview / Hook)

Most companies I talk to have heard of NIS2 but have no idea what it actually requires.

Here's the short version:

NIS2 is a European Union cybersecurity directive — and it's not asking whether your systems are secure.

It's asking: **do you have the protocols in place to handle it when something goes wrong?**

That means:
- Do you have an incident reporting process?
- Do you have a risk analysis framework?
- Can you document who does what, when, and how?

It's less about firewalls and passwords, more about organizational readiness and accountability.

132 requirements across 12 categories. Every essential sector. Every EU member state.

If your company is in scope, you need to be able to show your work — not just do the work.

## Post 2 — Who does NIS2 apply to?

"Does NIS2 apply to us?"

It's the first question I get from almost every company I speak to.

Here's the rule of thumb:

**Sector + Size = Scope**

**Sectors covered:**
- Transportation
- Healthcare & pharma
- Banking & financial institutions
- Digital infrastructure
- Manufacturing (in some cases)
- Food processing
- Waste management
- Cybersecurity software providers
- ...and more

**Size threshold:**
- 50+ employees, OR
- €10M+ in annual revenue

So if you're a 10-person company making €5M in an essential sector — you're likely out of scope.

But if you're above either threshold in one of these sectors — you're in.

Each EU country is implementing NIS2 through its own national agency (in Germany, that's the BSI). The rules are EU-wide, but enforcement is local.

## Post 3 — The 12 categories of NIS2 requirements

NIS2 has 132 requirements. But they're organized into 12 clear categories.

Here's what you're actually being asked to manage:

1. **Governance & Liability** — Who is accountable at the top?
2. **Risk Management** — How do you identify and assess threats?
3. **Incident Handling** — What's your response process?
4. **Business Continuity** — Can you keep running under attack?
5. **Supply Chain Security** — Are your suppliers also compliant?
6. **Procurement & Development** — Are security practices baked into how you build and buy?
7. **Effectiveness & Assessment** — Are you testing and improving?
8. **Cyber Hygiene & Training** — Do your employees know what a phishing email looks like?
9. **Cryptography & Encryption** — How is sensitive data protected?
10. **Access Control & HR** — Who has access to what, and why?
11. **Authentication & Communications** — Are authentication standards met?
12. **Registration & Reporting** — Are you registered with your national authority?

Each category has 8–10 subcategories. Each one needs a documented protocol, a responsible person, and an evidence trail.

The goal isn't perfection — it's proof.

## Post 4 — The incident reporting timeline (most companies get this wrong)

One of the most misunderstood parts of NIS2 is the incident reporting timeline.

Here's exactly what's required when a significant incident occurs:

- **Within 24 hours** → Early warning to your national authority (in Germany: the BSI)
- **Within 72 hours** → Full incident notification with details
- **Within 30 days** → Final incident report

Three deadlines. All start from the moment you become aware of the incident.

Most companies aren't ready for this. Not because they lack good people — but because they haven't defined the process *before* the incident happens.

NIS2 doesn't just want you to respond well. It wants proof that you had a plan.

That means documented procedures, assigned roles, and an audit trail that shows the right people did the right things at the right time.

## Post 5 — What auditors actually look for

When a NIS2 auditor shows up, they're not going to hack your systems.

They're going to ask to see your documentation.

Specifically:
- Who is responsible for each requirement?
- What protocol does your company follow?
- When was it last reviewed?
- Can you show me the evidence?

That's it. NIS2 compliance is fundamentally a documentation and process discipline — not just a technical one.

The companies that will struggle aren't the ones with bad security. They're the ones with good security that can't prove it.
